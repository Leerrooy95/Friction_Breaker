The first thing critics are missing is that this isn't one problem — it's a stack of problems designed to reinforce each other. There are laws that give enormous tax breaks to datacenters. There are laws that let utility companies charge your electricity bill for infrastructure built to serve those datacenters — before the datacenters are even open. There are laws that strip your city or county of the right to decide where these massive industrial facilities get built. And there are laws that make it harder to fight back through ballot initiatives. Each of these is a separate mechanism, and critics tend to attack one while the others keep operating. A speech about tax fairness doesn't fix a rigged utility rate structure.

The second thing being missed is the race-to-the-bottom trap. More than 37 states are competing to attract the same datacenters by offering bigger and bigger subsidies and fewer and fewer rules. Any single state that tries to add accountability requirements risks losing the project to a neighbor with fewer rules. This means state-level opposition — even when it wins — often just moves the problem next door. The only fix for a race to the bottom is a national floor: federal minimum standards that apply in every state, so no state can undercut the others. Bernie Sanders introducing federal minimum standards for datacenter subsidies — requiring beneficial ownership disclosure, environmental review, and binding power purchase agreements before ratepayers can be charged — would be the single highest-leverage action available. It changes the structural incentive rather than fighting one deal at a time.

The third missing piece is who actually owns these facilities. Billions of dollars are flowing into AI datacenter infrastructure through layered corporate structures that legally conceal the ultimate owner. It is entirely possible — and documented in related contexts — that foreign sovereign wealth funds hold significant positions in infrastructure being subsidized by American taxpayers and powered by American ratepayers, with no public disclosure required. Requiring beneficial ownership disclosure as a condition of any public subsidy is a simple, popular, bipartisan idea that would immediately change the accountability landscape. It can be done at the state level without waiting for federal action.

Finally, critics are focusing on the wrong finish line. The most durable damage from the current datacenter buildout may not be the tax breaks — those can eventually be repealed. It may be the water rights being quietly allocated in economic development agreements, and the proprietary infrastructure lock-in that makes entire state economies permanently dependent on a small number of technology vendors. These are not reversible by a future legislature in the way a tax exemption is. The opposition that wants to actually get ahead of this issue needs to be analyzing what these deals look like in 20 years, not just what the press release says today.
